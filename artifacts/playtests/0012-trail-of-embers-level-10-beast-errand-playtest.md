# Trail of Embers Level 10 Beast Errand Playtest

Date: 2026-07-13
Build committed: `fa54aa7` — Add Trail of Embers Level 10: The Beast Errand
Tester: Ian Chia
Prototype: Trail of Embers
Status: Playtest evidence, not source-of-truth ratification

## Playtest Context

Ian manually played Level 10, **The Beast Errand**, which combines Cinder Cache / Cinder Charge, Beast Gate, and One-Way Passages. This artifact preserves his feedback from that playtest. It does not tune the level, ratify any mechanic, or establish a post-Level-10 design direction.

## Ian's Reported Feedback

- **Understanding the level:** Ian said working things out is a big part of the game. Because the player cannot see much, they use the light they have and try to work out what the level requires. He was ultimately able to do that, felt the level was in line with the game, and called it a good level.
- **One-way commitment:** The commitment was clear. It created a moment of second-guessing whether to go through because Ian knew he could not come back, while also understanding that he might have to commit.
- **Beast Gate:** It felt like **luring the beast to operate the switch**.
- **Cinder Charge:** Ian did not collect it on this level.
- **Left-route teaching beat:** It barely registered.
- **Tension peak:** The most tension was at the gate while luring the beast.
- **Interest and pacing:** Ian felt there was enough added variation for the level to remain interesting. His broader warning was that the game needs more added material or it will get stale quickly.
- **Deaths:** No deaths felt confusing or unfair; everything happened as expected.
- **Three-mechanic cohesion:** Ian did not give a distinct direct answer about whether the mechanics felt like one situation or three mechanics placed in the same level.
- **Biggest desired change:** Ian's answer was broader than a single change to Level 10. He said it was a good level, but the game needs to keep building, adding complexity, and becoming more interesting, fun, or challenging.

## Evidence-Supported Findings

- The one-way passage created hesitation and commitment pressure in this playtest.
- The Beast Gate read as luring the beast to operate the switch, and the gate/luring sequence was the tension peak.
- Ian completed the level without collecting the Cinder Charge.
- The left-route teaching beat barely registered for Ian.
- Death outcomes behaved as Ian expected.
- Ian considered the level good and consistent with Trail of Embers.
- Ian believes the game will become stale quickly if it does not continue to grow in complexity, interest, fun, or challenge.

## Open Implications and Questions

- Ian's completion without the Cinder Charge is evidence about this run only. It does not prove that the charge is balanced or optional across players.
- Because the left-route teaching beat barely registered, its role and readability remain open questions. This result does not by itself establish that Level 10 should be tuned.
- Whether the three mechanics felt like one unified situation remains unanswered; no conclusion should be inferred from Ian's other comments.
- Ian's concern about staleness points toward investigating how the game can keep growing. It does not establish that a new mechanic is necessary or ratify any particular solution.

## Manager Interpretation and Recommendation

The manager currently interprets Level 10 as a successful synthesis probe. This is manager interpretation, not Ian's verbatim playtest evidence.

- Preserve Level 10 as-is for now.
- Do not immediately tune it.
- Investigate how Trail of Embers should grow after Level 10 without simply adding another puzzle rule.

## Status

This note preserves bounded playtest evidence. It does not change project truth, ratify the combined mechanics, prescribe Level 10 tuning, or approve a new mechanic.
