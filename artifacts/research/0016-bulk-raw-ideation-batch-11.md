## Concept 1

**Concept name:** The Presentation

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where players pitch competing proposals to a shared goal, then vote on which to execute — but the person who proposed the winning idea doesn't score, only the people who voted for it do.

**Core player action:** Propose a plan from a limited hand of "angle" cards, then argue for it using only constraints (budget, time, ethics) — not benefits. Vote secretly for which proposal you'd actually execute.

**Beginner appeal:** The premise is immediately recognizable — anyone who's been in a meeting or a group decision gets it. First move is clear: read your angles, pick one that seems defensible, say something.

**Mastery source:** Learning to propose plans that others will vote for *without* making them so attractive that you're suspected of angling for the win yourself. Skilled players learn to propose plans that benefit the voters, not the proposer — and to detect when someone is doing that.

**Replayability driver:** The "angle" deck changes each game; the shared goal is randomly drawn (e.g., "open a restaurant," "plan a festival," "save a failing company"); different player counts shift the voting math entirely.

**Social / watchability hook:** Watching someone argue passionately for a plan they clearly don't want to win, while everyone tries to read whether that's what's happening. The reveal of who voted for what is a mini-narrative.

**Likely failure mode:** Analysis paralysis on proposals. If players spend too long crafting their pitch, the game bogs down. Mitigation: strict time limits per proposal phase.

**Smallest probe:** Four players, eight index cards with simple angles (e.g., "fast," "cheap," "safe," "flashy"), one shared goal ("plan a birthday party"). Play two rounds without scoring — just see if the dynamic of proposing vs. voting produces tension.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only (works as conversation game), not competitive-only (has collaborative voting phase), not cooperative-only, no literal hunting, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic (angles could be tokens or roles), no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system.

## Concept 2

**Concept name:** The Echo

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where players secretly write a short instruction, pass it to the next player who interprets it through a different sensory mode (draw what they read, act what they see, describe what they acted), and the last player must guess the original instruction — but you score by how far your instruction *changed*, not by accuracy.

**Core player action:** Write an instruction that is likely to mutate interestingly through three transformations (text→image→movement→speech). You want it to become something recognizably derived but meaningfully different.

**Beginner appeal:** The game is basically Telephone with modalities. Everyone knows Telephone. First turn: write any simple instruction. No wrong answer — mutation is the goal.

**Mastery source:** Learning which instructions survive transformation intact (boring) versus which produce beautiful or hilarious mutations (good). Skilled players learn to write instructions that have ambiguous words, visualizable actions, or structural puns that generate specific kinds of shifts.

**Replayability driver:** The instruction deck (or freewriting) is infinite; different player orders produce different mutation chains; scoring rewards variety, so repeating a known "good" instruction eventually scores less.

**Social / watchability hook:** The reveal of each transformation chain is inherently theatrical — watching someone try to act out a drawing of a description of an instruction. The audience sees every step of the mutation.

**Likely failure mode:** Players who can't draw or act feel excluded. Mitigation: all modalities are optional — you can always describe instead of draw, or write instead of act. The game should celebrate bad drawings and ham acting.

**Smallest probe:** Three players, no materials. Player 1 writes an instruction on paper (e.g., "stand on one foot"). Player 2 reads it and acts it out silently. Player 3 watches the act and describes aloud what they think the instruction was. Compare. Try five rounds, no scoring — just observe whether mutation patterns are interesting.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only (works with just paper and bodies), not competitive-only (can be cooperative scoring), not cooperative-only (can be scored competitively), no literal hunting, no animals, no cards (instructions are written), no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system.

## Concept 3

**Concept name:** The Scaffold

**One-sentence pitch:** Players take turns placing a single block on a shared structure — but you don't control where your block goes; you control the *rule* for where the next player must place theirs, and the structure collapses if any rule is impossible to follow.

**Core player action:** On your turn, announce a placement rule (e.g., "must touch exactly two existing blocks," "must be placed at least one block-width away from the nearest red block," "must be placed lower than the highest block"). The next player must place their block obeying all rules currently in effect. If no legal placement exists, the structure collapses and everyone but the rule-maker scores.

**Beginner appeal:** Jenga-like physicality plus simple rule logic. First move: place a block and say "must touch this block." Immediately clear what's happening.

**Mastery source:** Learning to set rules that are possible *now* but will become impossible in 2-3 turns, while appearing generous. Skilled players create nested constraints — rules that seem independent but intersect fatally later. Also: reading whether a rule you're about to set accidentally makes the current position illegal already.

**Replayability driver:** The ruleset is emergent every game; different block shapes (cubes, planks, cylinders) change the possibility space; player order determines who gets to set the final constraint.

**Social / watchability hook:** The tension of watching someone try to find a legal placement while knowing the rule-maker is watching for the collapse. The moment of "wait, that's impossible" is a shared dramatic beat.

**Likely failure mode:** Physics frustration — the structure collapses from bad stacking, not rule impossibility. Mitigation: use a grid-and-marker system instead of physical blocks (place tokens on a hexagonal grid where "support" means adjacency, not physics). Or accept that physics adds chaos but is part of the fun.

**Smallest probe:** Two players, six identical tokens (coins or stones), a flat surface. Player 1 places a token and says "must be adjacent to my token." Player 2 places a token obeying that, then sets a new rule "must be at least two spaces from any token." Player 1 places obeying both. Continue until someone can't. See if interesting constraint chains emerge in under 5 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only (physical but not tabletop-game format — no board, no cards, no printed rules), not competitive-only (scoring can be cooperative variant), not cooperative-only, no literal hunting, no animals, no cards, no map/board (the surface is not a board), no combat, no economy/resource system.

## Concept 4

**Concept name:** The Witness

**One-sentence pitch:** A game played entirely in silence where each player has a hidden objective about how the group should arrange itself in physical space, but no one can speak, point, or write — they can only move, stop, or shift their gaze.

**Core player action:** On each turn, every player simultaneously either moves to a new position in the room, stops moving, or shifts their gaze to look at a specific other player. The round ends when everyone has stopped moving. Points are awarded based on whether the final arrangement satisfies your hidden objective (e.g., "be the northernmost person," "have at least two people between you and the door," "be looked at by the most people").

**Beginner appeal:** No rules explanation beyond the objective card. First action: just move somewhere. The silence creates immediate presence.

**Mastery source:** Learning to read others' movement patterns to infer their objectives, then using your own movement to either satisfy your goal, block theirs, or misdirect. Skilled players learn to use gaze timing — looking at someone to draw attention away from your actual target, or holding still early to suggest you've already achieved your goal.

**Replayability driver:** Large pool of objective cards; different room layouts; different player counts change the space geometry and social density.

**Social / watchability hook:** Pure physical theater. Watching six people slowly orbit each other in silence, making eye contact, stopping, shifting — it's visually compelling. The reveal of objectives afterwards produces "aha" moments about why someone moved where they did.

**Likely failure mode:** Awkwardness or discomfort with silence and physical proximity. Mitigation: keep rounds very short (30 seconds max); allow players to "pass" by sitting down; frame as a game, not an exercise.

**Smallest probe:** Three players in a small room. Give each a slip of paper with one simple objective (Player A: "be closest to the door"; Player B: "be looked at by someone at the end"; Player C: "have no one between you and a wall"). Play one 20-second round in silence, then reveal. Does the silence generate tension or just confusion? Try three rounds.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only (physical, no components), not competitive-only (can be scored cooperatively), not cooperative-only (hidden objectives are competitive), no literal hunting, no animals, no cards (objectives on slips of paper), no map/board, no combat (no conflict, just positioning), no economy/resource system.

## Concept 5

**Concept name:** The Archive

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where you collectively reconstruct a forgotten event by answering questions about it — but each answer you give removes that question from the deck forever, and you score points for the *rarity* of the questions you answer correctly.

**Core player action:** Draw a question from the dwindling deck about a shared event that no one fully remembers (a party, a meeting, a journey). Answer it as best you can from fragments of memory (handed out as secret clues at the start). The question is then discarded permanently — no one else will ever get to answer it. You score more if few other players could have answered it.

**Beginner appeal:** The premise is inherently human — "we all experienced something, but we remember it differently." First turn: draw a question, check your memory fragments, say what you think happened. No wrong answers, just different accuracy.

**Mastery source:** Learning to infer which questions are rare (and thus high-value) from the wording, and saving your best memory fragments for those questions. Also: learning to answer in ways that are *just specific enough* to score without revealing information that helps opponents on later questions.

**Replayability driver:** The event is randomly generated from modular elements (who, what, where, when, why) at the start of each game; the question deck is large and drawn without replacement; memory fragments are dealt randomly, so every game has different knowledge distribution.

**Social / watchability hook:** The collective reconstruction is a story in itself — watching the group piece together what happened from partial, sometimes contradictory memories. The moment someone answers a question nobody else could is a small victory.

**Likely failure mode:** Knowledge asymmetry feels unfair if one player gets the "key" memory fragment. Mitigation: fragments are redundant — multiple players may have overlapping information; the game is about who answers *when*, not who knows the most; the scoring rewards claiming rare questions, not being right about everything.

**Smallest probe:** Three players, 10 index cards with questions about a fictional event (e.g., "The Company Picnic of '22" — questions like "Who brought the potato salad?" "What color was the banner?"). Each player gets 3 memory fragments on separate cards (e.g., "You remember someone arguing about mayonnaise"). Play through all 10 questions, discarding each after it's answered. Does the dwindling deck create urgency? Is the scoring (rarity-based) intuitive?

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only (cards work but could be verbal), not competitive-only (can be cooperative reconstruction), not cooperative-only (scoring is competitive), no literal hunting, no animals, cards are used but as question prompts, not primary mechanic (memory fragments could be verbal), no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system (memory fragments are not resources — they're knowledge states).
