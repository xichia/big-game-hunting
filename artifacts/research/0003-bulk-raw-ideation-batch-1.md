## Concepts

Concept name:
The Pulse

One-sentence pitch:
A collective rhythm game where players synchronize taps on a shared surface to build a heartbeat, but secretly compete to subtly shift the tempo and claim credit for the downbeat.

Core player action:
Tap on the current beat, or perform a "break" gesture (open palm) to change the beat's direction or speed, forcing others to adapt.

Beginner appeal:
Tapping along to a shared beat is primal, zero-rules, and instantly intuitive; anyone can jump in without explanation.

Mastery source:
Learning to accelerate or decelerate the beat just enough to break an opponent's rhythm while staying locked in yourself; hiding your intent to break until the last millisecond; reading micro-tensions in the group's tap pressure.

Replayability driver:
Every round generates a unique emergent groove; player personalities create different group rhythms; changing group size drastically changes the feel and strategy.

Social / watchability hook:
Visually and auditorily captivating — watching a group achieve flow state or collapse into chaos is mesmerizing for spectators; the moment someone "steals" the downbeat is dramatic and audible.

Likely failure mode:
Caveman brain takes over and it becomes a boring, predictable metronome if players lack courage to disrupt; requires a strong incentive to push tempo boundaries.

Smallest probe:
4 players around a table. One shared surface. Establish a target downbeat. Player 1 starts a tap pattern. Play passes clockwise. After 3 minutes, see if a competitive dynamic for "control" of the beat naturally emerges.

Forbidden assumptions avoided:
No digital-only or tabletop-only (works raw in a circle). No cards, map, board, combat, economy, hunting, or animal theme. Blends competitive and cooperative elements.

Concept name:
Corridor

One-sentence pitch:
A spatial bluffing race where players move through a shared 3D tower of passes and dead-ends, secretly placing obstacles in each other's paths *after* seeing where they committed to move.

Core player action:
Place your pawn on a new node in the tower, then secretly draw a "void" token from a bag to place in the path of the player on your left.

Beginner appeal:
Move a pawn up a tower; put a wall in someone's way. The physical action is intuitive and the competitive tension is immediately legible.

Mastery source:
Building long-term double traps that span 3 rounds; reading which paths opponents are defending to know where they are vulnerable; sacrificing a strong position to bait a rival into a dead-end; memory of void token distribution.

Replayability driver:
The tower's connection graph is generated differently each game; the player meta shifts drastically between aggressive and defensive play styles.

Social / watchability hook:
Loud table flares: "No! I knew you were going to block me there!" The springing of a trap set multiple turns ago creates a huge spectator payoff.

Likely failure mode:
Analysis paralysis if void tokens are too open; player hard-locking if surrounded (mitigated by limited void tokens and alternative branches).

Smallest probe:
3 players. Paper cups stacked into a 3×3×3 pyramid as the tower. Pawns are coins. Walls are paperclips. Play for 5 turns and observe if a satisfying trap cycle emerges.

Forbidden assumptions avoided:
No traditional map or board (stacks of cups). No cards as primary mechanic (bag of tokens). No combat, economy, digital bias, literal hunting, or animal theme.

Concept name:
The Turing Test

One-sentence pitch:
A conversation game where players answer prompts in rigid "bot speak" while secretly trying to signal a partner through intentional, deniable glitches.

Core player action:
Answer a prompt in exactly 5 words. If you break the letter of the bot script or get caught glitching by a non-partner, you lose standing. If you successfully glitch your partner, you gain standing.

Beginner appeal:
The "AI vs. Human" frame is immediately understood. Speaking like a robot is funny and easy. First rounds are pure comedy and require no strategic depth to enjoy.

Mastery source:
Crafting a glitch that looks like an accidental stumble but perfectly encodes the secret target; recognizing an ally's glitch amid noise; faking a glitch perfectly to frame another player.

Replayability driver:
Role rotation combines with changing prompt categories (describe, list, yes/no, rate) and rotating bot scripts; the social comedy changes dramatically with each group.

Social / watchability hook:
Extremely reactive. "DID YOU SAY TECH COMPANIES?! YOU WEREN'T SUPPOSED TO!" The public reveal of who was signaling whom is explosive.

Likely failure mode:
Too subjective if the group can't agree on what qualifies as a plausible "accident"; requires a clear standard of evidence for a glitch.

Smallest probe:
4 players. 2 are bots. Bots must answer in exactly 5 words. One has a secret partner. Prompt: "Name an emotion." Does the partner successfully receive the signal within 2 rounds?

Forbidden assumptions avoided:
Not digital-only or tabletop-only (can be played in a circle). No cards, map, board, combat, economy, hunting, or animals. Team element exists but is not standard social deduction.

Concept name:
Negotiator

One-sentence pitch:
A zero-resource trading game where the only currency is binding promises that constrain your future actions — the winner is the player who best values what they haven't yet given away.

Core player action:
Offer a binding written promise to another player ("I will not place my token on X spot next turn"). They give you a vote token in return. You must trade to act.

Beginner appeal:
Humans naturally trade promises. "I'll do this if you do that" is universal. The promise is written down, removing ambiguity on enforcement.

Mastery source:
The value of a promise is 100% contextual; masters know when a promise will expire in value, frame large-sounding promises that cost them little, and read who is bluffing about their needs.

Replayability driver:
The meta-game of trust and bluff is unique with every group. The negotiation phase is the entire game. Player bargaining personality defines every session.

Social / watchability hook:
Theatre of the mind. "I PROMISE I won't vote against you next round." "BUT YOU PROMISED!" Extremely high drama and cross-table whispering.

Likely failure mode:
If players are too trusting, the game becomes a boring logistical exercise; if too cynical, no one trades. Requires a strong "must trade to act" rule to force engagement.

Smallest probe:
3 players. You need an action token to place a piece. You can only get an action token from another player by offering them a written promise. Play 5 rounds. Does a story of debt and betrayal emerge?

Forbidden assumptions avoided:
No economy (promises are constraints, not resources). No cards as primary mechanic. No map, board, combat, digital assumption, natural theme, or hunting frame.

Concept name:
Affect / Receiver

One-sentence pitch:
A silent signaling game where players place colored tokens representing reactions into a shared timeline, scoring points when their intention is correctly interpreted by the group based purely on context.

Core player action:
Place a colored token (Red / Blue / Yellow / Green) into a column of the timeline that represents the *strength* of your reaction (weak to strong) to the previous player's placement.

Beginner appeal:
Colors map to universal emotional categories (Agree / Disagree / Question / Elaborate). The shared context of the sequence makes reads intuitive even for a first-timer.

Mastery source:
Building a consistent "color language" with opponents; using an ambiguous color in a high-strength column to create a double bind; playing a meta-move that comments on the state of the entire conversation history.

Replayability driver:
The meaning of colors can shift per round. The adjacency matrix of the timeline expands and changes shape. Every group develops its own unique language.

Social / watchability hook:
Very abstract and visually beautiful. The tension of "will they get it?" — "Oh my god, they placed RED there, they must be furious with me." High stress and high payoff.

Likely failure mode:
Complete ambiguity — if no player can consistently read another's intent, scores flatline and frustration rises.

Smallest probe:
3 players. 4 colored tokens (Red = Agree, Blue = Disagree, Yellow = Question, Green = Elaborate). A 6-slot timeline. Each turn, place a token in a slot. The next player must guess the intended meaning. Does a consistent communication pattern emerge within 10 placements?

Forbidden assumptions avoided:
No cards, map, board, combat, economy, digital-only, tabletop-only, hunting, or animal theme. Scoring is consensus-based, blending competitive deduction with cooperative language-building.
