Concept name: The Echo Chamber

One-sentence pitch: Players take turns placing sound tokens on a grid to create interference patterns, scoring points for silent zones while disrupting opponents.

Core player action: Place a token that emits a sound wave, claim a silent zone on the grid, rotate your resonator to shift the interference pattern.

Beginner appeal: First move is obvious — place a token near the center; immediate visual feedback from pattern changes; scored zones are clearly marked.

Mastery source: Predicting interference 2-3 turns ahead, forced moves that create dead zones for opponents, timing a resonator rotation to flip a contested zone.

Replayability driver: Each game's pattern evolves uniquely; player order and resonator positions create new geometries every round.

Social / watchability hook: Spectators see interference patterns grow and collapse; "I can't believe you just silenced his zone" moments are dramatic.

Likely failure mode: First-mover advantage if center placement dominates.

Smallest probe: 6×6 paper grid, two colored tokens, two paper resonators. Two players. Play 3 turns each, scoring silent zones.

Forbidden assumptions avoided: No digital requirement, no tabletop-only assumption (works on any surface), not competitive-only (cooperative variant possible where players jointly maximize total silent zones), no map/board restriction (grid is not a map), no cards, no combat, no economy.

Concept name: The Stained Glass

One-sentence pitch: Players compete to lay colored transparent tiles over a light box, creating overlapping color blends; the player whose color dominates the final image wins.

Core player action: Snap a tile onto an existing tile's edge, covering or revealing light through color combinations.

Beginner appeal: Place tiles anywhere on the growing structure; color mixing rules are intuitive (blue+red=purple); always a valid placement.

Mastery source: Forward planning of which tile positions will become trapped, forcing opponents into low-value placements, setting up a final reveal where your color floods the image.

Replayability driver: Tile shapes, color order, and starting seed create infinite variation; optimal strategy depends on the current composition.

Social / watchability hook: The final image is revealed at the end — dramatic visual reveal; "I thought I was losing until the light hit my yellow tiles" stories.

Likely failure mode: If tie score resolution is unclear or if placement becomes trivial.

Smallest probe: 3 colored paper squares (red, blue, yellow), one flashlight, two players. Take turns stacking squares; after 5 stacks, shine light through and count which color dominates the projection.

Forbidden assumptions avoided: No digital, no tabletop-only (physical light works), not competitive-only (co-build an image), not cooperative-only (can be competitive scoring), no cards, no economy, no combat.

Concept name: The Turnstile

One-sentence pitch: Players bid for the right to move their token forward on a circular track, but the bid is paid in position — the highest bidder loses ground.

Core player action: Secretly choose a bid number from your hand of tokens; highest bid moves forward but discards tokens equal to the gap between your bid and the second-highest.

Beginner appeal: Obvious first move — bid 1 to move forward safely; no complex resource management.

Mastery source: Reading opponent tendencies to underbid, overbid to force costly wins, timing a zero-bid to preserve tokens for a later surge.

Replayability driver: Bidding dynamics shift each round; players adjust to each other's style; token economy creates escalating tension.

Social / watchability hook: "He just paid 4 tokens to move 1 space — brilliant or terrible?" creates discussable moments; visible token piles show who is rich or desperate.

Likely failure mode: If the optimal strategy is always to bid 0 or always to bid maximum.

Smallest probe: 10 tokens per player, a paper track of 20 spaces, 2 players. Play 5 rounds, recording bids and positions.

Forbidden assumptions avoided: No digital, not tabletop-only (could be app or conversation), not competitive-only (cooperative variant: all must reach the end together), no cards (tokens), no map (track is a line, not a map), no combat, no economy (bidding is positional, not resource-based).

Concept name: The Weather Vane

One-sentence pitch: Players collectively build a wind pattern by placing directional arrows; each player scores points when their secret wind direction aligns with the prevailing flow.

Core player action: Place one arrow on the grid, pointing any direction, then rotate one existing arrow by 90 degrees.

Beginner appeal: Place arrows anywhere; scoring is revealed after each round; secret wind direction is a simple choice (N/S/E/W).

Mastery source: Predicting which direction opponents will secretly align to, setting up arrow chains that shift the prevailing wind, baiting opponents into rotating arrows away from your direction.

Replayability driver: Secret directions change each round; arrow placement creates emergent patterns; player reads become the core skill.

Social / watchability hook: The final reveal of secret directions creates surprise; "I thought you were East but you were West the whole time" — memorable misdirection.

Likely failure mode: If one direction is always dominant, or if the scoring system is too opaque for beginners.

Smallest probe: 4×4 grid, 8 arrows on paper slips, two players. Each secretly chooses a direction. Place arrows for 4 rounds. Score when prevailing direction (most arrows pointing same way) matches your secret.

Forbidden assumptions avoided: No digital, not tabletop-only (arrows could be physical objects), not competitive-only (cooperative: all try to match a single direction), not cooperative-only (competitive scoring), no cards, no economy, no combat.

Concept name: The Sundial

One-sentence pitch: Players place shadow-casting objects on a light plane; scoring zones appear and disappear as the light source moves, rewarding those who predict where shadows will fall.

Core player action: Place a vertical object on the grid, then after each round, rotate the light source by one position, moving all shadows.

Beginner appeal: Place objects anywhere in the light; shadows are visible immediately; scoring zones are clearly marked.

Mastery source: Predicting shadow paths across multiple rotations, placing objects at the edge of current light to create long shadows later, forcing opponents' objects into self-shadowing positions.

Replayability driver: Light rotation sequence can be randomized or player-chosen; object placement creates unique shadow geometries each game.

Social / watchability hook: Watching shadows sweep across the grid is visually engaging; "Your shadow just covered my scoring zone" moments are clear and dramatic.

Likely failure mode: If shadow geometry is too complex to predict, or if optimal placement is always at the grid center.

Smallest probe: A desk lamp, paper grid, 4 paper cylinders. Place cylinders, rotate lamp 90 degrees after each round. Score zones under shadows.

Forbidden assumptions avoided: No digital, not tabletop-only (physical light works), not competitive-only (cooperative: maximize total shadow coverage), not cooperative-only (competitive scoring), no cards, no economy, no combat.
