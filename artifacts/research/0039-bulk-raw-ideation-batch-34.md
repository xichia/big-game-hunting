## Concept 1

**Concept name:** Voxel Doubt

**One-sentence pitch:** A real-time trust game where two players share control of a single character but each privately decides whether to move toward shared goals or their own secret objective.

**Core player action:** Privately input a direction or action for the shared character; the system resolves only the intersection of both players' inputs — if they disagree, nothing happens.

**Beginner appeal:** The rules are immediately graspable — you want to go somewhere, you press a direction. The tension comes from watching the character freeze or move in unexpected ways, which is inherently funny and engaging.

**Mastery source:** Learning to read your opponent's patterns, predict their objectives, and signal your intentions through partial compliance — all without direct communication. Experts learn to intentionally "fail" in ways that reveal opponent intent.

**Replayability driver:** The secret objective deck is large enough that no two games have the same win condition; the meta-game of learning a specific opponent's tells across multiple rounds creates escalating depth.

**Social / watchability hook:** The shared character's hesitant, stuttering movement is inherently comedic; spectators see both players' inputs on a split screen, creating dramatic irony — they know what each player is trying to do while the players don't.

**Likely failure mode:** Analysis paralysis — if both players freeze waiting for the other to commit, the game stalls. Mitigation: a countdown timer per move.

**Smallest probe:** Two players, a shared grid drawn on paper, two pencils. Each privately circles a destination cell. On each turn, both point to an adjacent cell; if they point to the same cell, the shared marker moves there. Play 6 turns; the player whose secret destination is closest to the final marker wins.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only (works on paper), not tabletop-only (digital is better but not required), not competitive-only (cooperative variants natural), not cooperative-only (core is competitive), no literal hunting, no animals, no cards (secret objectives can be drawn from a hat), no map/board (a simple grid suffices), no combat, no economy.

## Concept 2

**Concept name:** Echo Chamber

**One-sentence pitch:** A conversation game where players make claims about the world, and each claim's truth value depends on how many other players echo it — but players score by being both echoed and original.

**Core player action:** On your turn, state a true-or-false claim about a shared topic. Other players privately vote "believe" or "doubt." The claim becomes "true" if a majority believes it, "false" otherwise. You score if your claim's voted status matches its actual truth value — but you also score bonus points if your claim was the only one voted "true" this round.

**Beginner appeal:** The mechanic is intuitive — you say something, people vote if they believe it, and truth is determined by the group. No rules overhead beyond understanding scoring.

**Mastery source:** Reading the room's knowledge level, predicting what others will believe, and deliberately choosing between safe high-probability claims (likely to be believed correctly) and risky original claims (likely to be believed incorrectly, giving you the originality bonus when you're right).

**Replayability driver:** Topic decks (science, history, pop culture, local trivia) ensure variety; the group's collective knowledge evolves across sessions, changing what is "predictable."

**Social / watchability hook:** The moment where a player states something obviously false, everyone votes "doubt," and the moderator reveals it was actually true — creating a collective "aha" moment. These reversals generate shareable stories.

**Likely failure mode:** Groups that are too knowledgeable make "believe" votes too predictable, collapsing the scoring tension. Mitigation: include a "confident doubt" mechanic where voting doubt on a true claim gives you a point.

**Smallest probe:** Four friends, a shared topic (e.g., "animal facts"), a stack of trivia statements. Each player takes three turns stating claims; other players vote thumbs up/down. Score as described. Play in 15 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only (pure conversation), not tabletop-only (needs no components), not competitive-only (cooperative variant where group tries to maximize collective score), not cooperative-only (core is competitive), no literal hunting, no animals, no cards (though trivia statements are card-like, they're not a primary mechanic), no map/board, no combat, no economy.

## Concept 3

**Concept name:** The Last Crossing

**One-sentence pitch:** A survival migration game where each player leads a group across a dangerous landscape, but the only resource you can spend is permission — you let others go first, or you force your way ahead.

**Core player action:** On each leg of the journey (river, mountain pass, forest), players secretly bid "yield" or "push." If you yield, you wait while others go first — you arrive later but with full strength. If you push and someone else also pushes, you clash and both arrive damaged. If you push and everyone yields, you arrive first and unscathed.

**Beginner appeal:** The binary choice (yield or push) is immediately clear and emotionally resonant — players instantly understand the tension between cooperation and competition.

**Mastery source:** Learning to read opponent patterns, establishing reputation (are you always a pusher or can you be trusted to yield?), and understanding when arriving early is worth the risk of damage versus when arriving later but intact is strategically superior.

**Replayability driver:** The journey has variable leg lengths (3–7 legs per game); different "group compositions" create different payoffs for arriving early vs. late at each leg; players can form temporary alliances to guarantee safe passage.

**Social / watchability hook:** The reveal moment — seeing who pushed and who yielded — creates instant narrative. "I thought you were going to yield!" becomes a recurring phrase. Spectators can track reputation arcs across multiple games.

**Likely failure mode:** If yielding always dominates, nobody pushes and the game becomes a slow cooperative march. Mitigation: arriving first at certain legs grants permanent advantages (extra survival, secret knowledge) that make pushing valuable.

**Smallest probe:** Five players, a list of 5 "legs" on paper, each player has a token. Each leg: players secretly choose "yield" or "push" by hiding a colored token in their fist. Reveal simultaneously. Push vs. push = both skip leg (damaged). Push vs. all yield = pusher advances. All yield = all advance. First to reach the end wins. Play 3 rounds.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only (pure paper/token), not tabletop-only (could be conversation-based), not competitive-only (cooperative variant natural), not cooperative-only (core is competitive), no literal hunting, no animals, no cards, no map/board (a linear track is not a map), no combat (clash is not combat), no economy.

## Concept 4

**Concept name:** Provenance

**One-sentence pitch:** A deception game where players claim ownership of artifacts, and the "truth" of ownership is determined by who can tell the most consistent story about the artifact's history.

**Core player action:** An artifact is revealed. Each player, in turn, adds one sentence to its "provenance" (the chain of ownership). You must either extend the current story coherently OR claim the artifact as yours by stating "and it has been in my family's possession since [event]." If you claim, all other players vote: does the full story hold together? If yes, you win. If no, you're eliminated.

**Beginner appeal:** No rules beyond "tell a story" and "vote on whether it makes sense." The creative act of storytelling is intrinsically appealing and requires no game literacy.

**Mastery source:** Learning to build stories that are plausible, consistent, and trap others into extending them in ways that make their own claims unbelievable. Advanced players learn to leave subtle contradictions for others to accidentally violate.

**Replayability driver:** The artifact deck contains varied items (mundane and exotic) that demand different storytelling strategies; the meta-game of learning what your specific group considers "consistent" evolves over time.

**Social / watchability hook:** The best moments are when a player claims ownership with a brilliantly coherent story that everyone believes — or a player overreaches and the story collapses in laughter. The storytelling format is inherently performative and shareable.

**Likely failure mode:** Groups that are too generous (believing any story) or too strict (rejecting all stories) break the game. Mitigation: a calibrating round with sample artifacts and known-good/bad provenance chains.

**Smallest probe:** Three players, a deck of 10 index cards each with a random object written on it (shoe, crown, telescope, etc.). Players take turns as "keeper" who draws a card and starts the provenance with one sentence. Next player adds a sentence. Game proceeds as described. Play until someone successfully claims or all players are eliminated.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only (pure conversation and paper), not tabletop-only (needs no table), not competitive-only (cooperative group-storytelling variant), not cooperative-only (core is competitive), no literal hunting, no animals, no cards (though index cards carry prompts, the mechanic is not card play), no map/board, no combat, no economy.

## Concept 5

**Concept name:** The Committee

**One-sentence pitch:** A game of collective decision-making where each player is secretly trying to steer the group toward their own "pet issue" while appearing to be a neutral, reasonable committee member.

**Core player action:** A problem is presented to the committee. Players propose solutions one at a time. After each proposal, the committee votes yes/no. If a proposal passes, the game ends and that player's secret issue either advances (if aligned with the solution) or is blocked. If no proposal passes after all players have proposed, the default solution (which blocks everyone's pet issue) takes effect.

**Beginner appeal:** The premise ("you're on a committee trying to get your thing done") is universally relatable and immediately understood. The tension of pretending to be fair while pushing a hidden agenda is intrinsically fun.

**Mastery source:** Learning to craft proposals that genuinely solve the presented problem (so others vote yes) while subtly favoring your pet issue. Advanced play involves building coalitions, making credible threats to block others' proposals, and knowing when to sacrifice your issue to block a rival's.

**Replayability driver:** The problem-and-issue deck has combinatorial variety; player reputation carries across games, creating ongoing rivalries and alliances; the meta-game of learning what your group considers "reasonable" or "selfish" evolves.

**Social / watchability hook:** The moment a player's hidden agenda is revealed — "You were pushing solar power THIS WHOLE TIME?" — creates dramatic reversals. Spectators can track the subtle cues of who is pushing for what.

**Likely failure mode:** If players always vote no on any proposal that isn't theirs, nobody can pass anything and the game collapses into the default solution. Mitigation: a "compromise token" that allows a player to force one element of their issue into any proposal they support.

**Smallest probe:** Four players, each given a secret "pet issue" written on a slip of paper (e.g., "fund schools," "build roads," "protect parks," "cut taxes"). Present a shared problem. Players take turns proposing a one-sentence solution. After each proposal, vote by show of hands. If majority yes, the game ends and revealed issues are checked. If all proposals fail, default solution applies. Play 3 rounds with fresh issues.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only (pure conversation), not tabletop-only (needs no materials), not competitive-only (cooperative committee variants exist), not cooperative-only (core is competitive), no literal hunting, no animals, no cards (issue prompts are not a card mechanic), no map/board, no combat, no economy.
