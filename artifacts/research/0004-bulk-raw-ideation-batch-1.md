## Concept 1

**Concept name:**  
Crowd Sourcing

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players collectively build a shared structure (a tower of tokens, a drawing, a list of rules) but each secretly wants the final result to favor their hidden criteria.

**Core player action:**  
Add one element to the shared structure each turn, following current constraints set by previous contributions.

**Beginner appeal:**  
It is immediately clear: place your token; the hidden goal adds curiosity without confusion.

**Mastery source:**  
Learning to read how others are shaping the structure and timing your additions to push toward your goal without revealing it.

**Replayability driver:**  
The hidden goals are drawn from a deck each game, producing different tensions each time; the shared structure also resets completely.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
Players groan or cheer as someone tilts the tower or colors inside unexpected lines; observers can guess what each player might be aiming for.

**Likely failure mode:**  
Hidden goals can be either too easy to guess (reducing the hidden‑information tension) or too abstract (players feel no control).

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, six identical tokens, three secret goal cards (e.g. “make the tallest token stack” / “make the stack lean left” / “middle token red”); players take turns adding one token; after ten tokens score by secret goal; use paper and colored pencils.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
Digital-only, tabletop-only, competitive-only, cooperative-only, literal hunting, animals, cards as primary, map/board, combat, economy/resource system.

## Concept 2

**Concept name:**  
Beat the Boot

**One-sentence pitch:**  
A rhythm‑matching game where players tap a shared sequence of beats but constantly change which beat they are responsible for.

**Core player action:**  
Tap the current beat of a shared pulse, then pass the responsibility for that beat to another player.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Everyone can tap a rhythm; the rules are two sentences long.

**Mastery source:**  
Anticipating when a player will dump a difficult beat on you and timing your own dumps to create patterns you can predict better than others.

**Replayability driver:**  
The beat sequence can be generated by any simple random method (dice, ambient noise) or by a player‑designed pattern, so no two games sound the same.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The game becomes a live performance; watchers can hear who is fumbling and who is smooth; memorable “almost dropped the beat” moments.

**Likely failure mode:**  
Players with strong musical rhythm dominate too heavily; silence might be awkward for shy groups.

**Smallest probe:**  
Two players, a metronome app (or tapping a table at a steady BPM), a token passed after each beat; each player must tap on their beat and pass; record who fails to tap in time; after 30 beats, the one with fewer misses wins.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (digital-only, tabletop-only, etc. – no components beyond a timer or app, no map, no cards, no resource, no combat, no animals).

## Concept 3

**Concept name:**  
Liar’s Dice Without Dice

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players secretly assign values from a small pool to slots in a sequence, then bid on the order of those values without ever revealing their own.

**Core player action:**  
Claim a rank order for the hidden values, using only public information and logical deduction.

**Beginner appeal:**  
The concept is familiar from bluffing games; assigning values is a simple choice.

**Mastery source:**  
Crafting claims that look plausible while forcing opponents into contradictions; reading the gap between what someone claims and what they must have.

**Replayability driver:**  
The small pool of values and the number of slots means each hand is a fresh deduction puzzle.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The bidding phase resembles a poker table without money; observers can try to deduce the hidden assignment alongside the players.

**Likely failure mode:**  
Rounds can become deterministic if the value pool is too small or the deduction track too short; the game may feel solved after a few plays.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, six numbered cards (two 1’s, two 2’s, two 3’s) placed face down in a line; each player secretly writes which card they think is where; simultaneously reveal; score points for correct guesses; no dice, no board, no combat; play 5 rounds.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
Digital-only, tabletop-only, competitive-only, cooperative-only, literal hunting, animals, cards as primary (cards are used but as a simple numbered token, not a primary mechanic – the core is deduction, not card play), map/board, combat, economy/resource system.

## Concept 4

**Concept name:**  
Turn the Screw

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Every round the turn order reverses, skips, or accelerates based on where a single “pressure token” sits among the players.

**Core player action:**  
Choose to pass or keep the pressure token, knowing it will affect who acts next and how many actions they get.

**Beginner appeal:**  
One token, one decision: keep for advantage or pass to avoid penalty; the turn‑order chaos is immediately exciting.

**Mastery source:**  
Calculating how many future tempos you sacrifice by holding the token versus how much you gain by controlling the action sequence.

**Replayability driver:**  
The pressure token moves in a different pattern each game, creating varying alliances and vendettas.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The moment someone holds the token too long triggers groans and laughter; spectators can track the token’s path and predict meltdowns.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If keeping the token is always beneficial, nobody ever passes and the dynamic collapses.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, one coin (the pressure token), a simple action deck of 6 cards (e.g. “draw a token from the bag”); each turn the player with the token chooses to keep it and act twice, or pass it to the left and act once; after 8 turns, the player with most tokens collected wins; test with pen and paper.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (no map, cards used but only as action prompts – not the primary mechanic; no combat, no resources, no animals, etc.).

## Concept 5

**Concept name:**  
Word Weight

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Each round a common word is revealed and players place secret bets on how many other players will also choose that word to define a shared concept.

**Core player action:**  
Write down a single word that you believe will match the majority answer for a given category.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Picking a word is trivial; the betting mechanic adds a simple “which answer is most popular” puzzle.

**Mastery source:**  
Predicting not the obvious answer, but the answer that exactly enough other players will choose – and calibrating your risk between safe and distinctive.

**Replayability driver:**  
The categories are infinite and player‑suggested, so the game never repeats.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The reveal is an explosion of laughter when only one person wrote “spork” for “kitchen utensil”; great party audience game.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If answers are always the obvious majority word, betting becomes trivial; need a push toward divergence.

**Smallest probe:**  
Four players, a list of 10 categories (e.g. “a fruit”, “a verb”), each player secretly writes one word per category; then guess how many matched; score based on correct bets; paper only, 15 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (no components beyond pen and paper; no competitive‑only – can be cooperative scoring variant; no map, combat, resources, etc.).

## Concept 6

**Concept name:**  
Chain of Custody

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players pass a single token around a circle, each modifying its “value” slightly, then score based on how the token ends up relative to each player’s secret target.

**Core player action:**  
Add +1, –1, or nothing to a numeric token before passing it to the next player.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Only three choices per turn; immediate cause and effect visible on the token’s number.

**Mastery source:**  
Reading the chain momentum – knowing when to dampen or accelerate changes to land near your own target while pulling it away from others’.

**Replayability driver:**  
Secret targets are drawn from a large range each game, so the same numeric chain produces different winners.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The token’s number is public; spectators can see each player’s subtle sabotage and predict who might win as the chain moves.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If the token’s range is too small, the game becomes random; if too wide, mastery feels out of reach.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, one token starting at 10, each player secretly draws a target (e.g. 7–13); on your turn add –1, 0, or +1 to the token and pass; after three full cycles, whoever’s secret target is closest to the final token value wins; play with paper and a single die or token to track number.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (no map, no cards as primary, no combat, no resources, no animals, etc.).

## Concept 7

**Concept name:**  
Hollow Pawn

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Each player controls a “pawn” but the board is the set of all players’ minds – you move by asking questions that force others to reveal their position.

**Core player action:**  
Ask a yes/no question about a hidden arrangement; based on the answer, you can claim a space or reposition your pawn.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Just ask a question and move a marker; the hidden arrangement is small enough to hold in memory.

**Mastery source:**  
Crafting questions that split the remaining possibilities optimally while hiding your own target from opponents eavesdropping.

**Replayability driver:**  
The hidden arrangement can be generated from any limited set (numbers, colors, shapes) and players can suggest the theme.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The questioning phase is high tension – every answer reveals something to everyone; spectators can play along in their heads.

**Likely failure mode:**  
Optimal questioning can be solved by information theory, turning the game into a dry logic puzzle.

**Smallest probe:**  
Two players, a set of six hidden items (e.g. three colored tokens under three cups), each player secretly chooses one as their goal; players alternate asking yes/no questions about the arrangement (e.g. “Is red next to blue?”); after five questions each, they must guess the arrangement; correct guess wins; no board, no map, no combat.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (uses questioning, not cards, no economy, etc.).

## Concept 8

**Concept name:**  
Quick & Quiet

**One-sentence pitch:**  
A round‑based game where only the quietest player scores, but each round the scoring condition shifts (closest to a target, most unique, etc.) based on the previous round’s result.

**Core player action:**  
Choose a number (or word) secretly in response to a prompt.

**Beginner appeal:**  
The action is always just writing one thing down; the shifting scoring is a fun surprise.

**Mastery source:**  
Predicting not only what others will answer, but how they will adapt to the scoring shift – and modulating your own answer to fit the next scoring condition.

**Replayability driver:**  
The scoring condition evolves per game in a branching tree depending on player choices, so no two sequences feel the same.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
Seeing a player accidentally walk into the “loudest” punishment when they thought they were being safe is hilarious; observers can bet on the next condition.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If the scoring rules are not well balanced, one branch dominates and choices feel limited.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, each round says a number 1–10; after all reveal, the scoring rule is determined by a simple rule like “smallest unique” or “closest to 7”; write each rule on a slip; the first rule is random; after each round, the next rule is chosen from a set of 3 by the previous round’s winner; play 10 rounds; paper only.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (no cards, no map, no combat, no resources, etc.).

## Concept 9

**Concept name:**  
Back‑Seat Driver

**One-sentence pitch:**  
One player is “blind” and navigates a path by following instructions from other players, who have conflicting preferred destinations.

**Core player action:**  
Give the blind player a movement instruction (step left, step right) while hiding your true preferred destination.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Everyone understands guiding someone; the twist of secret preferences is immediately graspable.

**Mastery source:**  
Learning to give ambiguous instructions that help the navigator advance but also nudge toward your destination without being detected by other guides.

**Replayability driver:**  
The navigator’s goal (or the scoring system) can change each game – sometimes the navigator is trying to reach a specific spot, sometimes avoiding traps.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
Watching the blindly stumbling navigator and the disguised tug‑of‑war between guides is theatrically compelling; great for audience participation.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If the guides can fully control the navigator, the game becomes a boring direct‑control drill; if the navigator has too much free will, guidance feels pointless.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players: one blindfolded (or eyes closed) stands on a starting point; the other two each have a secret target location marked on the floor (with tape); on each turn both guides whisper one direction (N/S/E/W) to the blind player, who must follow one of them (cannot hear both? Actually use simultaneous whispers then player chooses which to obey? Or public directions? For probe: each guide says a direction aloud, blind player chooses which to follow; after 10 moves, see who is closer to which target; score points accordingly; measure distance with tape; no board beyond floor).

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (physical movement, no cards, no map, no combat, no resources, etc.).

## Concept 10

**Concept name:**  
Token Pool

**One-sentence pitch:**  
All players draw from a single shared pool of tokens, but every time you draw you must also discard one of your own, and the tokens have different values depending on how many of each color remain.

**Core player action:**  
Choose to draw a token from the pool, then choose which token from your hand to discard back.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Simple rule: draw one, discard one; the changing values are easily observed.

**Mastery source:**  
Calculating the future value of tokens based on what you think others will draw, and timing your draws to maximize end‑round scoring.

**Replayability driver:**  
The token pool composition changes each game based on the initial setup and player choices; endless variety.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The tense moment when someone draws the last of a color, crashing its value; observers can’t resist counting tokens.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If one color is always best, the game collapses into a race; need careful design of value function.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, a pool of 15 tokens in 3 colors (5 each), each token color’s value = number of that color left in the pool; players take turns drawing one token and discarding one from their own collection back to the pool; after 12 draws, each player scores the values of tokens they hold; paper score tracking.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
Digital-only, tabletop-only, competitive-only, cooperative-only, literal hunting, animals, cards as primary, map/board, combat, economy/resource system (the tokens could be any physical items; the mechanic is not a full economy, it’s a simple scoring pool).

## Concept 11

**Concept name:**  
Silent Auction

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players write blind bids for how much they are willing to pay, but the “currency” is the right to speak during the next round of negotiation.

**Core player action:**  
Secretly bid a number of “talk tokens” (slips of paper) for the chance to argue for your position; highest bidder wins the floor, but spends those tokens.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Auction mechanics are familiar; the resource (talk tokens) is simple and visual.

**Mastery source:**  
Deciding when to spend heavily to push your agenda and when to conserve, reading opponents’ willingness to stay silent.

**Replayability driver:**  
The topic of negotiation can be anything the group decides (where to eat, what to watch, who takes the last cookie), so the game fits any context.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The auction itself is performative; watching a player blow all their tokens to win a trivial argument and then go silent is comedy gold.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If the auction always goes to the same player, the game becomes a boring one‑person show; need balanced incentive to not overbid.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, each starts with 5 talk tokens (pennies). A simple decision: collectively choose a number between 1 and 10. Players bid tokens secretly; highest bid gets to argue for 30 seconds for their preferred number, then a vote; if their number is chosen they win something; if not, they wasted tokens. Play three decisions; test with paper score.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (no map, no cards as primary, etc.).

## Concept 12

**Concept name:**  
Twisted Ladder

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players ascend a metaphorical ladder by completing tasks, but the rungs’ order is secretly shuffled by a rotating “ladder keeper” who also wants to win.

**Core player action:**  
Declare which rung you are attempting, then perform the required mini‑task (e.g., rhyme a word, balance a coin) – but the keeper may have moved that rung’s requirement.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Simple to try a rung; the keeper’s treachery is obvious and fun.

**Mastery source:**  
Predicting the keeper’s shuffling pattern, and learning when to attempt a rung before it changes again.

**Replayability driver:**  
The mini‑tasks can be drawn from a huge set; the keeper role rotates each round.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The keeper’s sly smile as they swap two rungs during their turn is highly entertaining; audiences cheer when a player guesses right.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If the keeper’s power is too strong, players may feel they have no agency.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, a list of 6 tasks written on separate cards face down in a row (rung 1–6). The keeper rotates each round; the keeper may secretly swap two rung cards; the active player picks a rung number, then must perform the task on the card now at that position; success moves them up; first to reach rung 6 wins; test with paper slips and pen.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (cards used as task prompts, not primary mechanic; no map, combat, etc.).

## Concept 13

**Concept name:**  
Echo Chamber

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Each round one player starts a story with a sentence, and each subsequent player must repeat the story so far before adding their own sentence – with scoring based on accuracy and creativity.

**Core player action:**  
Repeat the entire accumulated story aloud, then add a new sentence.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Storytelling is natural; the memory challenge is immediate and fun.

**Mastery source:**  
Weaving your new sentence in a way that reshapes the story to your advantage while making it easy for others to remember (or hard, if you want them to slip).

**Replayability driver:**  
The starting prompt can be anything; the story branches wildly each game.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
Hilarious breakdowns when someone misremembers a key detail; spectators can follow the drift of the narrative.

**Likely failure mode:**  
The game may become too hard for casual groups if the story gets long; need a reset mechanism (e.g., after 5 players, start a new chain).

**Smallest probe:**  
Four players, no materials. First player says a sentence. Second repeats it and adds one sentence. Third repeats both and adds one. Continue for three rounds. Score one point per correctly repeated sentence. Play several rounds with different starting sentences.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (pure spoken game, no components).

## Concept 14

**Concept name:**  
Magnetic Drift

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players secretly place “attract” and “repel” markers on a line of locations, then a single token moves according to the net forces, and players score based on where it stops.

**Core player action:**  
Place one attract + one repel on any empty locations, trying to pull the token toward your own scoring zone.

**Beginner appeal:**  
The physical analogy (magnet push/pull) is intuitive; placement is just picking two spots.

**Mastery source:**  
Predicting the net force after all players’ placements and learning to counter opponents’ magnets without overcommitting.

**Replayability driver:**  
The line length and scoring zones can be reconfigured; player count changes the force calculations.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
Watching the token slide and seeing whose magnets sway it at the last moment is visually satisfying.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If placement is too symmetrical, the token always lands in the middle; need asymmetric goals.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, a line numbered 1–10 on paper, a token (coin) starting at 5. Each player secretly writes one attract (moves token one step toward that number) and one repel (moves one step away). Reveal simultaneously; compute net movement; token moves; after 5 rounds, players score points if the token is in their personal scoring zone (each assigned a 3‑number zone); test with paper.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (no map beyond a line, no cards, no resources, no combat).

## Concept 15

**Concept name:**  
Pressure Cooker

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players collectively inflate a shared pressure gauge by taking risks, but whoever causes it to burst loses everything.

**Core player action:**  
Roll a die and choose to add the result to the pressure gauge or pass, hoping others will take the risk.

**Beginner appeal:**  
One die, one gauge; the risk‑reward is immediately clear.

**Mastery source:**  
Reading the group’s risk tolerance and knowing when to push and when to fold – and manipulating others into bursting.

**Replayability driver:**  
The gauge threshold changes each game (maybe set by a previous round’s outcome), so the same die results feel different.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The screams when someone finally pushes the gauge over the edge are memorable; observers can shout advice.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If the gauge bursts too often or never, the game becomes either frustrating or boring; need careful threshold balancing.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, a die, a gauge starting at 0. On each turn the player may roll and add the value to the gauge, or pass (gain a small point). If gauge reaches 20, whoever made it burst loses a point; all others gain. Passers also gain. First to 5 points wins. Test with paper to track gauge and points.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (dice, no cards, no map, no economy beyond simple points, no combat, no animals, etc.).

## Concept 16

**Concept name:**  
Flip the Script

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Each player has a set of “scene cards” but the twist is that the same cards are also a shared resource – taking a card for your scene removes it from the public pool.

**Core player action:**  
Choose one public card to add to your personal scene, aiming to create a coherent story or pattern according to a secret role.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Drafting cards is straightforward; the secret role adds a puzzle element.

**Mastery source:**  
Anticipating which cards others need and either taking them or leaving traps; reading the emerging scenes.

**Replayability driver:**  
The secret roles and card sets can be infinitely varied; the drafting dynamic changes with player count.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The reveal of each player’s scene and role at the end is a reveal show; watching someone build a completely different story from the same pool is fascinating.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If the card pool is too small, choices feel forced; too large, the game loses tension.

**Smallest probe:**  
Three players, a pool of 12 card‑sized slips each with a word (e.g., “castle”, “storm”, “king”). Each player secretly draws a role card like “build a tragedy” or “build a comedy”. Players take turns drafting one card from the pool and adding to their personal area. After 8 total picks, each arranges their cards into a 4‑card scene and presents their story; best story according to role wins. (Cards are just word prompts; not primary mechanic – the core is drafting with hidden direction.)

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (cards used as prompts, not as a card game mechanic; no map, combat, etc.).

## Concept 17

**Concept name:**  
Tag Team

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Two players are physically linked (e.g., by holding a string) and must coordinate to move tokens on a track, but they cannot speak – and the other pair is racing opposite.

**Core player action:**  
Pull or slacken the string to signal your partner when to move, without verbal cues.

**Beginner appeal:**  
The physical link is novel and fun; the goal is simple: move your token to the end.

**Mastery source:**  
Developing a non‑verbal code with your partner while reading the opponent pair’s signals; timing your pulls to confuse opponents.

**Replayability driver:**  
Different pairs form different codes; the track layout can change (obstacles, alternate routes).

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The silent communication is hilarious to watch; pairs often break into laughter when signals misfire.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If one pair develops a very efficient code, they dominate and the game becomes a boring sprint.

**Smallest probe:**  
Two pairs (4 players), each pair holds a 1‑meter string between them. A simple track of 10 squares on paper; each pair has a token that advances when both players pull simultaneously. They must signal without speaking; the first pair to reach the end wins. Test with a coin and paper track; time limit per round.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (physical coordination, no digital, no cards, no map, no combat, no resources, no animals).

## Concept 18

**Concept name:**  
Pyramid Scheme

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players build a pyramid of tokens, but the value of each token depends on how many tokens are stacked above it, and players take turns placing or removing tokens.

**Core player action:**  
Either add a token to the top of any column or remove the top token from a column, changing the score for all tokens below.

**Beginner appeal:**  
The stacking is tactile and visual; the simple choice of add/remove is immediate.

**Mastery source:**  
Calculating the cascading score changes multiple moves ahead and forcing opponents into positions where removing a token hurts them more than you.

**Replayability driver:**  
The starting pyramid configuration can vary; the scoring formula can be tweaked for different strategic flavors.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The dramatic moment when a player removes a key token and several columns collapse in value; observers can see the ripple effect.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If removing always gives the highest score, the game becomes a demolition derby; need balance between building and tearing down.

**Smallest probe:**  
Two players, a base row of 4 tokens (any stackable objects like coins), then players alternately stack on top of any column or remove the top token of any column. Score = height of each column squared, summed. After all tokens are used (maybe 16 total), player with highest score wins. Test with paper and coins.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (no map beyond the stacks, no cards, no resources beyond tokens, no combat, no animals).

## Concept 19

**Concept name:**  
Catch & Release

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players collectively catch words from a fast‑spoken list, but each player wants to catch a different set – you must release words you don’t need and grab the ones you do.

**Core player action:**  
When a word is called, grab a token if you want to catch that word, or leave it if you’re filtering.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Instant reaction game; simple goal: catch the words on your list.

**Mastery source:**  
Listening for multiple words at once, prioritizing which words to fight for, and using body language to bluff about what you need.

**Replayability driver:**  
The word lists can be themed (animals, cities, verbs) and rotated; player roles can change each round.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The physical scramble for tokens is exciting to watch; the tension of a near‑miss grab.

**Likely failure mode:**  
If the word list is called too fast, it becomes purely a reaction test; if too slow, it’s boring.

**Smallest probe:**  
Two players, a set of word cards (or a reader). Each player secretly gets a target list of 3 words (e.g., “apple”, “chair”, “mountain”). The reader calls out random words from a pool every 5 seconds, and a token (e.g., a small object) lies in the middle. If the word is on your list, you grab the token to claim it. If not on your list, you must not grab. First to get all 3 target words wins. Test with paper word lists and any grab‑able object.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:**  
All forbidden assumptions (no cards as primary – just word lists; no map; no combat; fast physical game).

## Concept 20

**Concept name:**  
Bubble Wrap

**One-sentence pitch:**  
Players collectively pop a “bubble” (a point on a shared grid) each turn, but each player wants the final shape of popped bubbles to match a hidden pattern.

**Core player action:**  
Choose an unpopped bubble and pop it, adding its coordinate to the shared pattern.

**Beginner appeal:**  
Popping bubbles is satisfying; the hidden pattern gives personal direction.

**Mastery source:**  
Choosing bubbles that serve your pattern while not revealing your target; reading others’ patterns from their choices.

**Replayability driver:**  
The hidden patterns can be any small shape (letter, number, simple drawing); the grid size and pattern library can expand.

**Social / watchability hook:**  
The reveal of everyone’s patterns after the game
