**Concept name:** The Echo Chamber

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where players take turns placing statements into a shared conversation, then secretly bet on which statements will be repeated, contradicted, or distorted by later players.

**Core player action:** Play a statement card into the growing "echo chamber" timeline, then place secret prediction tokens on statements you believe will be echoed, contradicted, or ignored.

**Beginner appeal:** The premise is instantly recognizable — everyone has been in a conversation where ideas get repeated or twisted. First move is obvious: place a statement and guess its fate.

**Mastery source:** Reading the room — predicting not just which statements are sticky, but which specific players are likely to repeat them, and understanding the social dynamics of who echoes whom. Timing your predictions across the full conversation arc.

**Replayability driver:** Player groups develop their own culture and inside references. No two echo chambers form the same way. The meta-game of "what does this group tend to echo" evolves over repeated plays.

**Social / watchability hook:** The emergent narrative of which ideas survive, mutate, or die is inherently funny and dramatic. Spectators can participate by guessing alongside. Post-game "why did you repeat that?" conversations are the main event.

**Likely failure mode:** The game becomes about "gaming the scoring" rather than engaging with the conversation — players optimize for points instead of participating authentically, killing the social magic.

**Smallest probe:** Two rounds with 4-6 people, each with 3 blank cards. Everyone writes one statement per round. After placing, players secretly point at statements they think will be repeated. Reveal and discuss. No scoring, just observe: does the prediction moment create engagement?

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only (works in person with cards), not tabletop-only (could be digital), not competitive-only (has social/spectator elements), not cooperative-only, no literal hunting, no animals, no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system.

**Concept name:** The Long Count

**One-sentence pitch:** Players collectively build a multi-generational calendar by placing ritual stones, while secretly trying to ensure their own culture's "golden age" aligns with the most valuable celestial events.

**Core player action:** On your turn, either place a stone marking a year (adding to the timeline) or claim a celestial event for your culture by placing a token on a future predicted year.

**Beginner appeal:** Simple binary choice — add to history or stake a claim. The shared calendar is visible and physical. Beginners can place stones without understanding the strategy.

**Mastery source:** Manipulating the calendar's rhythm — spacing stones to create or deny favorable alignments for opponents' cultures, reading which years opponents will target, and bluffing about your own golden age conditions.

**Replayability driver:** Different celestial event decks create different scoring landscapes. Player count changes the calendar density. The "who-owns-which-era" dynamic shifts every game.

**Social / watchability hook:** The calendar grows visibly over the game, creating a shared artifact. Dramatic moments when a claimed event aligns or misses. Spectators can see the pattern forming.

**Likely failure mode:** Analysis paralysis from trying to calculate future alignments. The calendar becomes too abstract and loses the "generations" narrative feel.

**Smallest probe:** A shared timeline of 20 spaces on paper. Each player has 5 stone tokens and 3 claim tokens. No special events — just "closest to your stone" scoring. Play until all stones are placed. Takes 15 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only (has collaborative world-building), not cooperative-only (secret goals conflict), no literal hunting, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic, no map/board (timeline is not a map), no combat, no economy.

**Concept name:** The Fall

**One-sentence pitch:** In a crumbling tower where rooms collapse in a fixed sequence, players must arrange their belongings across the floors — but can only save what they can reach before the room they're standing in falls.

**Core player action:** Each round, secretly assign your three tokens to different rooms of the tower. When a room is announced as collapsing, any tokens there are lost. Players in that room must flee to an adjacent room, abandoning their remaining tokens.

**Beginner appeal:** The threat is clear and immediate — "don't be in the room that falls." The collapse sequence is shown upfront. First move is just placing tokens somewhere.

**Mastery source:** Deduction of opponents' token placement patterns, forcing them into bad positions by predicting where they'll flee, sacrificing your own position to trap opponents, and reading the collapse sequence to stage multi-turn escapes.

**Replayability driver:** Different tower configurations (number of rooms, adjacency patterns, collapse sequences) create distinct puzzles. Player count changes density and competition for safe rooms.

**Social / watchability hook:** The collapse announcements create natural suspense. Watching a player realize they're trapped is dramatic. Spectators can track who's where and predict the next collapse.

**Likely failure mode:** The game becomes solvable — optimal placement patterns emerge that are hard to disrupt, reducing it to memory and calculation rather than reads.

**Smallest probe:** 5 rooms in a line on paper. 2 players, 3 tokens each. Collapse sequence: rooms 1, 3, 5 in order. Play 3 rounds. Just observe: does anyone successfully trap someone else? How often is it obvious where to place?

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only, not cooperative-only, no literal hunting, no animals, no cards (tokens only), no map/board (rooms are a simple spatial abstraction), no combat (conflict is positional), no economy.

**Concept name:** The Quorum

**One-sentence pitch:** Players are factions in a committee trying to pass or block an agenda item, but nobody knows the full voting rule — each player has partial information about what determines a valid quorum.

**Core player action:** Each round, either call a vote (risking failure if you misjudge the quorum), reveal a clue about the quorum rule, or challenge another player's understanding of the rule.

**Beginner appeal:** The "what counts as a majority?" question is simple and intuitive. Beginners can play by revealing clues without understanding the full strategic depth. Voting is a familiar action.

**Mastery source:** Building an accurate model of the quorum rule from incomplete clues, deceiving opponents about your own understanding, timing your vote call when you're confident the quorum is met, and bluffing about your knowledge to block opponents' votes.

**Replayability driver:** Different quorum rules (majority of all players, majority of present players, two-thirds, unanimous, weighted by faction size, etc.) create completely different strategic landscapes. Players can learn the rule quickly but the meta-game of "who knows what" changes every game.

**Social / watchability hook:** The tension of "will this vote pass?" is the entire game. Spectators can track the revealed clues and try to deduce the rule themselves. Post-game reveals of the actual rule create "aha" moments.

**Likely failure mode:** The game becomes about who can solve the logic puzzle fastest, rewarding deduction over social play. Players sit silently calculating instead of engaging.

**Smallest probe:** 3 players, one secret rule from a deck of 6. Each player gets one clue. Play one "voting round" where each player can call a vote once. Do people call votes? Do they bluff? Takes under 10 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only, not cooperative-only, no literal hunting, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic (clues could be verbal), no map/board, no combat, no economy.

**Concept name:** The Palimpsest

**One-sentence pitch:** Players add layers of meaning to a shared blank surface by drawing symbols, then later layers can partially obscure or reinterpret earlier ones — but you score based on what remains visible at the end.

**Core player action:** On your turn, either draw a new symbol on the surface (in your color) or select an existing symbol and declare its "true meaning" (overwriting its original intent, but potentially obscuring it).

**Beginner appeal:** Drawing is inherently accessible. The action choice is simple: add or reinterpret. Beginners can draw without worrying about strategy.

**Mastery source:** Spatial planning — placing symbols where they'll survive later overwriting, forcing opponents to either waste turns obscuring your work or let your symbols score. Reading opponents' placement patterns to predict where they'll add or reinterpret.

**Replayability driver:** The physical palimpsest is different every game. Player styles and symbol choices create emergent art. The "what did that symbol mean?" debate after the game is its own entertainment.

**Social / watchability hook:** The evolving artwork is inherently watchable. Spectators can see the layers building. Dramatic moments when a clever reinterpretation changes the meaning of a whole region.

**Likely failure mode:** The game becomes about drawing skill rather than strategy. Players who can't draw well feel excluded. The scoring system becomes too subjective.

**Smallest probe:** A shared sheet of paper. 2 players, each with a pen color. Take turns drawing simple shapes (circles, squares, lines). After 3 shapes each, take turns "reinterpreting" shapes by circling them. No scoring — just observe: does anyone try to place shapes defensively? Takes 5 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only, not cooperative-only, no literal hunting, no animals, no cards, no map/board (the surface is a blank canvas), no combat, no economy.
