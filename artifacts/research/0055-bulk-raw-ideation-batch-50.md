## Concepts

**Concept name:** The Auction of Consequences

**One-sentence pitch:** Players bid for the right to choose the next public problem the whole group must solve, turning resource management into a tactical political game.

**Core player action:** Bidding tokens on "consequence cards" that define a global crisis (e.g., "Rations halved," "Movement frozen," "One player loses a turn"), then collectively solving it.

**Beginner appeal:** Very simple bid: raise a fist of tokens or pass. Immediate, dramatic outcome every round. No complex rules to memorize.

**Mastery source:** Reading opponents' risk tolerance, bluffing about your own resource position, and timing when to spend vs. hoard for later crises.

**Replayability driver:** Consequence cards are drawn randomly each game, creating unique crisis sequences. Bidding dynamics shift based on who is winning or losing.

**Social / watchability hook:** Every bid is a public performance—players negotiate, bluff, and react. The "who caused this disaster" moment is inherently dramatic.

**Likely failure mode:** The consequences become repetitive or too chaotic, leading to frustration rather than strategic tension.

**Smallest probe:** 3 players, 10 tokens each, 5 consequence cards from a pool of 10, one 15-minute round. Use paper slips for consequences and tokens.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Digital-only (no), tabletop-only (paper probe), competitive-only (hybrid with cooperative problem-solving), theme (no hunting/animals), cards as primary mechanic (consequences can be any medium), map/board (none).

**Concept name:** Echo Chamber

**One-sentence pitch:** Players whisper a phrase around a circle, competing to have their version be the one that survives intact, while deliberately corrupting others.

**Core player action:** Whispering a short phrase to one neighbor, then voting on which final spoken version is "the original" vs. a corrupted one.

**Beginner appeal:** Everyone knows the telephone game. No rules to learn beyond "whisper, listen, vote."

**Mastery source:** Strategic corruption—knowing which syllables, rhythms, or emotional tones are most fragile. Reading which players are honest vs. deceptive.

**Replayability driver:** Phrases are drawn from a deck each round, ensuring novelty. Players can develop rivalries and meta-strategies about who to trust.

**Social / watchability hook:** The final reveal where all versions are spoken aloud is hilarious and tense. Spectators can play along guessing the original.

**Likely failure mode:** If all players are equally skilled, rounds become random. Needs asymmetry in corruption ability to create skill differentiation.

**Smallest probe:** 4 players, 3 rounds, 5 phrase prompts written on paper slips. No whisper—just one-on-one oral transmission. Paper vote.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Digital-only (no), tabletop-only (no), competitive-only (no—can be cooperative variant), theme (no hunting/animals), cards (prompts can be slips), map/board (none).

**Concept name:** The Great Silence

**One-sentence pitch:** Players must cooperate to complete a sequence of tasks without speaking, using only pre-agreed gestures, while a saboteur tries to break the chain.

**Core player action:** Performing a physical gesture (e.g., tap shoulder, point, nod) to trigger the next task in a shared sequence, while watching for the saboteur's disruption.

**Beginner appeal:** Immediately intuitive—everyone has played silent games. The goal ("finish the sequence") is obvious.

**Mastery source:** Developing a subtle, variable gesture language that is robust against the saboteur. Reading the saboteur's tells without breaking the silence.

**Replayability driver:** Gesture language evolves each game. Players can create new signals, counter-signals, and fake-outs. Saboteur role rotates.

**Social / watchability hook:** The tension of a silent room where everyone is communicating non-verbally is inherently fascinating to watch. Mistakes are funny.

**Likely failure mode:** The gestural language becomes too complex to teach or remember, or the saboteur role is too easy/hard.

**Smallest probe:** 3 players, 1 saboteur, 5-task sequence written on a hidden card. 10-minute round. No materials except task cards.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Digital-only (no), tabletop-only (no—physical/body game), competitive-only (no—cooperative with hidden traitor), theme (no hunting/animals), cards (task prompts only), map/board (none).

**Concept name:** Architect of Fate

**One-sentence pitch:** One player designs a rule that governs how all players act for the next round, and everyone else must follow it perfectly or lose, creating a puzzle-like loop.

**Core player action:** The "Architect" writes a one-sentence rule (e.g., "Everyone must touch their nose before speaking"), then other players must execute it under scrutiny.

**Beginner appeal:** Extremely simple premise: one person makes a rule, everyone obeys. No complex scoring.

**Mastery source:** Architects must design rules that are ambiguous enough to trip opponents but clear enough to pass their own tests. Players learn to read rule loopholes.

**Replayability driver:** Infinite rule design space. Each round is a unique puzzle. The Architect role rotates, creating asymmetric challenges.

**Social / watchability hook:** The creativity of rule-making and the comedy of rule-breaking are inherently entertaining. "Did they break the rule?" moments.

**Likely failure mode:** Rules become too complex, arbitrary, or unfun, leading to frustration. Needs a strong "fun filter" for acceptable rules.

**Smallest probe:** 3 players, 1 Architect per round, 5 rounds. Paper and pen for rule-writing. No other materials.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Digital-only (no), tabletop-only (no—physical/verbal game), competitive-only (no—can be cooperative), theme (no hunting/animals), cards (none), map/board (none), economy (none).

**Concept name:** The Last Object

**One-sentence pitch:** Players secretly choose an object in the room to be "the last object," then take turns eliminating objects by describing a reason to discard them, trying to protect their chosen one.

**Core player action:** On your turn, name an object in the room and state a plausible reason to get rid of it (e.g., "The clock—it's too loud"). The group votes to eliminate or spare.

**Beginner appeal:** Zero setup. Everyone points at real objects. The social pressure of defending or attacking specific items is immediately understandable.

**Mastery source:** Choosing objects that are emotionally charged, ambiguous, or hard to attack. Reading the room to know when to attack vs. defend.

**Replayability driver:** The objects in the room change every game. Players develop reputations and rivalries around which objects they protect.

**Social / watchability hook:** The meta-game of "why are they defending that ugly lamp?" creates hilarious and revealing moments. Spectators can play along.

**Likely failure mode:** If the room is sparse or objects are too similar, choices become arbitrary. Needs a minimum object threshold.

**Smallest probe:** 3 players, a room with 10+ visible objects, 3 rounds. No materials. Play by conversation only.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Digital-only (no), tabletop-only (no—physical/location-based), competitive-only (no—can be cooperative variant), theme (no hunting/animals), cards (none), map/board (none), economy (none).
