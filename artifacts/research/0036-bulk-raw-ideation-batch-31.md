**Concept 1**

---

**Concept name:** The Echo Chamber

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where players secretly agree or disagree with provocative statements, and the group's averaged "echo" score determines which player earns the right to be contradicted.

**Core player action:** Each round, secretly vote whether you would publicly support or oppose a controversial statement (the statement is drawn from a neutral deck); then collectively average the votes to produce a group "Echo Score," and the player whose vote was closest to the average becomes the "Speaker" who must defend the statement publicly while all other players try to make them contradict themselves.

**Beginner appeal:** The voting is simple (yes/no), the statements are provocative and fun to react to, and the social pressure of being "the average" is immediately understandable.

**Mastery source:** Reading the room to predict the group's bias, knowing when to vote authentically versus strategically to become (or avoid becoming) the Speaker, and managing the tension between your true opinion and your performance as the Speaker.

**Replayability driver:** Each round's statement is different, player relationships and trust evolve across rounds, and the group's "culture" shifts over multiple plays.

**Social / watchability hook:** Watching someone defend a position they may not personally hold, with the group ganging up to trip them up, is inherently dramatic and funny.

**Likely failure mode:** Groups might default to polite agreement rather than generating interesting tension; the statement deck would need careful curation to be provocative but not alienating.

**Smallest probe:** Play a single round with 4 people, a single statement written on a napkin, secret yes/no votes by finger show, and a volunteer Speaker. No materials needed. Observe whether the round generates laughter, debate, and a desire to play another round.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** No digital-only, no tabletop-only, no competitive-only (it's semi-cooperative), no cooperative-only, no literal hunting theme, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic (votes are by hand gesture), no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system.

**Concept 2**

---

**Concept name:** The Alibi

**One-sentence pitch:** Each player claims to have witnessed a mundane event differently, and the group must reconstruct a single "true timeline" by challenging each other's accounts until only one player's story stands uncorrected.

**Core player action:** Each round, privately write down one detail of a shared fictional event (e.g., "The door was open" or "The dog barked twice"); then, in turn order, players read their detail aloud, and any player may challenge another's detail by claiming it contradicts what they heard earlier — if the challenger is wrong, they are eliminated from this round; if right, the challenged player is eliminated.

**Beginner appeal:** The premise is intuitive (people remember things differently), the challenge mechanic is simple (point out a contradiction), and the first few rounds are fast and low-stakes.

**Mastery source:** Crafting details that are plausible, internally consistent, and strategically placed to trap others; listening actively and building a mental model of the emerging timeline; timing challenges for maximum disruption.

**Replayability driver:** The "event" prompts can be varied infinitely (a party, a traffic accident, a wedding toast), the social dynamic shifts with each group, and players develop reputations for reliability or trickery.

**Social / watchability hook:** The tension of watching someone confidently state a detail that another player knows is false, waiting for the moment of accusation.

**Likely failure mode:** Games could stall if players are too timid to challenge, or devolve into arguing about logic rather than playing the game.

**Smallest probe:** 3 players, one shared event prompt spoken aloud ("a man bought coffee"), each player secretly writes one detail on scrap paper, then reads it aloud. If a player believes they hear a contradiction, they say "Contradiction" and explain why. Play until one player remains. Time: under 10 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** No digital-only, no tabletop-only, no competitive-only (it's elimination-based but social), no cooperative-only, no literal hunting theme, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic, no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system.

**Concept 3**

---

**Concept name:** The Annotator

**One-sentence pitch:** One player writes a short ambiguous story, then all other players take turns annotating it with questions and marginal notes, and the writer scores points for each annotation they can convincingly answer as if it were intended.

**Core player action:** The "Author" writes a 3-sentence story that is vague but not nonsensical; then each "Reader" in turn adds one annotation (a question, a note, or a correction) to the story; after all annotations are made, the Author must respond to each one out loud, and the group votes on whether each response is a plausible intended interpretation (Author scores a point) or a desperate retcon (no point).

**Beginner appeal:** Writing a short story is low-pressure, the annotation mechanic is familiar (think commenting on a document), and voting on plausibility is immediately satisfying.

**Mastery source:** Crafting a story that is vague enough to accommodate multiple interpretations but specific enough to anchor plausible responses; reading opponents' annotation styles to anticipate which ones are traps; improvising convincing explanations under social pressure.

**Replayability driver:** Stories and annotations are different every game, players develop a "voice" as annotators, and the meta-game of anticipating each other's annotation strategies evolves.

**Social / watchability hook:** Hearing someone creatively explain away a ridiculous annotation is entertaining; the group's vote on plausibility creates suspense.

**Likely failure mode:** The Author might feel unfairly judged if the group is too harsh, or the game could feel too cooperative if the group is too lenient.

**Smallest probe:** One person writes a 3-sentence story on a whiteboard or paper. Two others each add one annotation. The Author responds aloud. The group votes show of hands. One round only. Time: under 5 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** No digital-only, no tabletop-only, no competitive-only (it's scoring-based but creative), no cooperative-only, no literal hunting theme, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic, no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system.

**Concept 4**

---

**Concept name:** The Conveyor

**One-sentence pitch:** A silent cooperative game where players must pass a single object from one end of a line to the other using only verbal descriptions of what they want to happen, never touching the object themselves.

**Core player action:** Players sit in a line; the object (a token, a cup, a ball) begins at one end; the player at that end may not touch it, but must verbally instruct the next player in line to move it — but the instruction must be indirect ("I need the thing that is currently near me to be closer to the third person"), and the instructed player must interpret and act; if the object is dropped or misinterpreted, the team's score resets.

**Beginner appeal:** The premise is extremely simple (pass an object without touching it), the frustration is immediately funny, and the first few attempts are chaotic and memorable.

**Mastery source:** Developing a shared vocabulary of indirect commands (e.g., "The round thing that is nearer to the window should be moved toward the person with glasses"), learning each teammate's interpretation tendencies, and building collective timing.

**Replayability driver:** Different objects (different sizes, shapes, materials), different line configurations (different spacings, obstacles), and the team's improvement over time creates a natural progression arc.

**Social / watchability hook:** Watching the team's communication break down and then slowly improve is inherently dramatic; failed passes are hilarious.

**Likely failure mode:** The game could become frustrating if the group never improves, or too easy if they find a shortcut.

**Smallest probe:** 3 people in a line. One coin on the floor near the first person. The first person may not touch it, but must verbally guide the second person to move it toward the third person. If the coin is moved within 30 seconds, success. No materials beyond a coin.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** No digital-only, no tabletop-only, no competitive-only (fully cooperative), no cooperative-only (but this one is cooperative), no literal hunting theme, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic, no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system.

**Concept 5**

---

**Concept name:** The Silhouette

**One-sentence pitch:** One player strikes a pose behind a screen, and all other players must separately sketch the silhouette they imagine, then the group votes on which sketch best matches the actual pose.

**Core player action:** A "Model" strikes a pose behind an opaque screen so only their silhouette is visible; all "Artists" have 60 seconds to sketch the silhouette on paper; then the screen is removed, the Model holds the pose, and the group compares sketches to the real pose, voting on which is most accurate.

**Beginner appeal:** The activity is familiar (drawing a silhouette), the time pressure is exciting, and the comparison phase is immediately social and playful.

**Mastery source:** Learning to observe and reproduce shapes under time pressure, developing personal drawing shortcuts, reading the Model's likely body language, and strategic posing by the Model to be either clear or tricky.

**Replayability driver:** Different Models (different body types, flexibility), different poses (from simple to contorted), and artists improving over time.

**Social / watchability hook:** The disparity between sketches and the real pose is often hilarious; seeing someone's attempt to draw a complex pose is funny just for the effort.

**Likely failure mode:** Artistic ability could create an unfair advantage or discourage less confident players; the game could feel too similar to Pictionary.

**Smallest probe:** One person stands behind a piece of cardboard (or a held-up book). Two other players each have a piece of paper and a pen. The Model strikes a pose for 30 seconds, then the Artists sketch for 60 seconds. Compare sketches. One round. Time: under 3 minutes.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** No digital-only, no tabletop-only (it's physical/group activity), no competitive-only, no cooperative-only, no literal hunting theme, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic, no map/board, no combat, no economy/resource system.
