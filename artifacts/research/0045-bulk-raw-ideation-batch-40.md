**Concept name:** The Public Record

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where players compete to shape a shared historical narrative by submitting and voting on "facts" that become true, while trying to make their own contributions appear most credible.

**Core player action:** Propose a factual statement about the emerging history, then vote on which proposed facts enter the permanent record — balancing what helps your legacy against what the audience will believe.

**Beginner appeal:** The first move is obvious: propose something interesting about the world being built. Voting is intuitive — you pick which option you'd rather have be true.

**Mastery source:** Advanced players learn to read the room — understanding what the group will accept, seeding long-term narrative threads, creating backstories that benefit them several rounds later, and making proposals that serve multiple purposes simultaneously.

**Replayability driver:** No two histories will ever be the same. The emergent narrative is unique to each group's values, sense of humor, and strategic priorities.

**Social / watchability hook:** Watching the history unfold is inherently entertaining — absurd facts, dramatic reversals, and the tension of seeing what the group accepts produces moments worth retelling.

**Likely failure mode:** Analysis paralysis during proposal creation, or the game becoming a pure social exercise without strategic depth.

**Smallest probe:** Three players, ten blank index cards, two rounds of "propose a fact and vote" — test whether interesting decisions emerge before any complex scoring.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only (can be cooperative or semi-cooperative), not hunting-themed, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic (uses proposals and voting), no map/board, no combat, no economy.

**Concept name:** Echo Chamber

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where players whisper a message around a circle, but can try to intercept, amplify, or distort it — and score points based on how accurately their version spreads.

**Core player action:** When a message passes through you, choose to pass it faithfully, embellish it, truncate it, or replace it entirely — then try to deduce what the original actually was.

**Beginner appeal:** The telephone game is universally understood. Everyone knows how to whisper and how to mishear. First decision is simple: do I pass it on or change it?

**Mastery source:** Experts learn to encode messages that are hard to distort, predict which other players will alter messages and how, and read subtle cues in received messages to reconstruct the original — all while managing their reputation.

**Replayability driver:** Each round produces a unique chain of transformations. Different player counts and seating orders create different dynamics. The same starting message can end up unrecognizable or perfectly preserved.

**Social / watchability hook:** Watching the message mutate is hilarious. The reveal phase — where everyone sees the original vs. final — creates genuine surprise and laughter.

**Likely failure mode:** The game becomes purely random if players have no meaningful agency over the transmission quality.

**Smallest probe:** Five players, one sentence written on paper, whispered sequentially, then each player writes what they received — test whether players can meaningfully influence transmission without any additional mechanics.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only, not hunting-themed, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic, no map/board, no combat, no economy.

**Concept name:** The Long Game

**One-sentence pitch:** A patience-based game where players secretly commit to goals at the start, then spend the entire session making small moves that only pay off much later — with the winner being the one whose early investments were smartest.

**Core player action:** At each turn, choose between a small immediate benefit or a delayed effect that may or may not align with your secret long-term objective — but you cannot reveal what you're working toward until the end.

**Beginner appeal:** The immediate choices are concrete and low-stakes. Beginners can simply react to the current situation without worrying about long-term strategy.

**Mastery source:** Deep players learn to deduce others' secret objectives from their moves, disguise their own intentions, create false patterns to mislead opponents, and time their reveals perfectly.

**Replayability driver:** Different secret objectives create different optimal strategies. The meta-game of reading others and hiding your intentions changes every session.

**Social / watchability hook:** The endgame reveal — where everyone sees what each player was working toward — creates dramatic moments of realization and surprise.

**Likely failure mode:** Beginner frustration if immediate choices feel meaningless without understanding the long game, or the game becoming solvable if the set of possible objectives is too small.

**Smallest probe:** Three players, three secret goal cards, twenty rounds of "pick A for one point now or B for three points in five rounds" — test whether players can meaningfully disguise intentions with just this structure.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only, not hunting-themed, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic (uses tokens or simple tracking), no map/board, no combat, no economy.

**Concept name:** The Price of Certainty

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where players bet on how confident they are about upcoming uncertain events, but must reduce their confidence to act — creating a tension between being sure and being effective.

**Core player action:** Before each uncertain event resolves, declare how confident you are (from 0-100%), then decide how much of your confidence to "spend" on preparation — high confidence means you prepare well, but if you're wrong, you lose everything.

**Beginner appeal:** The core mechanic is intuitive — you're more willing to bet on things you're sure about. First decision is just picking a confidence level.

**Mastery source:** Experts learn to calibrate their confidence precisely, read probability signals others miss, bluff confidence to mislead, and manage the emotional pressure of high-stakes certainty.

**Replayability driver:** The uncertainty events can be anything — dice rolls, hidden information, other players' choices. Different event types create different strategic landscapes.

**Social / watchability hook:** The moment of resolution — when overconfident players lose everything or cautious players miss big opportunities — creates dramatic tension.

**Likely failure mode:** Players defaulting to always being 50% confident (avoiding both risks), making the game flat.

**Smallest probe:** Two players, a six-sided die, ten rounds of "declare your confidence in rolling above 3, then roll" — test whether the tension between confidence and action creates interesting decisions.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only, not hunting-themed, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic, no map/board, no combat, no economy.

**Concept name:** The Opposite Game

**One-sentence pitch:** A game where the goal is to lose — but secretly — and everyone must appear to be trying to win while actually working toward their own hidden failure condition.

**Core player action:** Execute moves that look like reasonable attempts to win, while subtly steering toward your personal failure condition — and try to deduce who else is secretly losing on purpose.

**Beginner appeal:** The surface game is straightforward ("try to win"). Beginners can play the surface game genuinely and still have fun, even if they don't grasp the hidden layer.

**Mastery source:** Experts learn to create convincing cover stories for suboptimal moves, identify the tells of other secret losers, and time their failure reveal for maximum confusion.

**Replayability driver:** Different secret failure conditions create different optimal strategies. The social deduction layer changes every session.

**Social / watchability hook:** The reveal — where everyone explains their real objective — produces "aha" moments and retroactive reinterpretation of earlier moves.

**Likely failure mode:** The game collapses if players don't understand or engage with the hidden layer, or if the surface game is too simple to sustain interest.

**Smallest probe:** Three players, a simple racing game (move toward a finish line), each with a secret goal like "finish third" or "never lead" — test whether the hidden objectives add depth without destroying the surface game.

**Forbidden assumptions avoided:** Not digital-only, not tabletop-only, not competitive-only, not hunting-themed, no animals, no cards as primary mechanic, no map/board, no combat, no economy.
